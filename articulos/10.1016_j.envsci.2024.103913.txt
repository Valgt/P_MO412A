Saha, Abhijit Mishra, Arunodaya Raj Rani, Pratibha Deveci, Muhammet Debnath, Bijoy Krishna Jamil, Norziana Mahmoud, Moamin A. 
                  Sustainable development serves as a guiding principle, striving to achieve human development goals while ensuring that natural systems can support essential ecosystem services and resources. The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, accepted by all United Nations Member States in 2015, offers a unifying framework for peace and prosperity for people and the planet, both now and in the future. The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) include specific targets and indicators, helping to assess a country's progress towards achieving the goals. To evaluate the EU (European Union) countries' alignment with the SDGs, this study develops a group decision-making approach by integrating DEA (Data Envelopment Analysis), MARCOS (Measurements Alternatives and Ranking according to COmpromise Solution), and an optimization model. A DEA is a highly suitable procedure for evaluating the performance of multiple peer entities, often referred to as Decision-making Units (DMUs) or alternatives. On the other hand, the benefits of MARCOS are: (i) it considers both the ideal (ID) and anti-ideal (AID) scenarios and, (ii) it exemplifies the utility degree of each option in association to ID and AID. Lastly, the combination of cross-entropy and divergence measures effectively deal with the information loss occurred during the determining the weights of considered criteria. Therefore, the proposed hybrid model is more sensible and practical. According to the final results, Austria stands out as the top performer among the EU countries in implementing the SDGs and achieving favorable outcomes.

Department of Industrial Engineering, Turkish Naval Academy, National Defence University Tuzla Istanbul 34942 Turkey       Sustainable development serves as a guiding principle, striving to achieve human development goals while ensuring that natural systems can support essential ecosystem services and resources. The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, accepted by all United Nations Member States in 2015, offers a unifying framework for peace and prosperity for people and the planet, both now and in the future. The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) include specific targets and indicators, helping to assess a country's progress towards achieving the goals. To evaluate the EU (European Union) countries' alignment with the SDGs, this study develops a group decision-making approach by integrating DEA (Data Envelopment Analysis), MARCOS (Measurements Alternatives and Ranking according to COmpromise Solution), and an optimization model. A DEA is a highly suitable procedure for evaluating the performance of multiple peer entities, often referred to as Decision-making Units (DMUs) or alternatives. On the other hand, the benefits of MARCOS are: (i) it considers both the ideal (ID) and anti-ideal (AID) scenarios and, (ii) it exemplifies the utility degree of each option in association to ID and AID. Lastly, the combination of cross-entropy and divergence measures effectively deal with the information loss occurred during the determining the weights of considered criteria. Therefore, the proposed hybrid model is more sensible and practical. According to the final results, Austria stands out as the top performer among the EU countries in implementing the SDGs and achieving favorable outcomes. However, this development has also led to significant global challenges that may threaten the well-being of future generations. The exponential growth of consumerism and the escalating impact of industrial production in the 1960s and 1970s have disrupted the natural balance and put immense pressure on the environment and natural resources (Blewitt, 2014 ). In response to these pressing issues, scientists and experts worldwide have emphasized the importance of ecological and environmental preservation, giving rise to the concept of sustainability. Originally focused on the relationship between development and the environment, the idea of sustainability has evolved to encompass all aspects of development, including economic, social, and environmental components (Rogers et al., 2012 ). The Brundtland report in 1987 defined sustainable development as meeting current needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs (Sachs, 2012; Hák et al., 2016; Pinto et al., 2017 ). While the goals pursued in the 1990s had their merits, today's objectives have shifted due to the impact of globalization, especially driven by advancements in information and communication technologies, leading to new economic, environmental, and social challenges (Elliott, 2012 ). The United Nations introduced the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) in 2000 to outline key priorities for sustainable development. These goals aimed to address issues such as environmental concerns, poverty, hunger, and gender inequality by 2015. Progress towards the MDGs varied among countries and objectives. As the MDGs reached their culmination, a new focus emerged on the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), embodied in the "Agenda 2030," which was agreed upon by UN Member States in 2015. The core of Agenda 2030 consists of 17 SDGs that call for global cooperation to enhance the global condition. The SDGs comprise 17 goals, with goals 1–6 carrying over from the MDGs and goals 7–17 addressing contemporary sustainability challenges. Each goal is associated with 5–12 targets, totaling approximately 303 indicators to monitor and assess progress towards the established objectives (Hák et al., 2016 ). To ensure the scientific validity of the goals and indicators, thorough development and evaluation discussions were conducted (Ritschelova, 2015 ; ICSU and ISSC, 2015 ; Pereira et al., 2021 ). Final findings suggest that about 17 % of the proposed targets may require revision (Hák et al., 2016 ), and the SD Solution Network (SDSN) has recommended 100 Global Monitoring indicators as reliable benchmarks. Some scholars caution that overlooking connections between the aims could lead to unintended and contradictory outcomes (Stafford-Smith et al., 2017 ). Eight years ago, the "Agenda 2030" was released, marking the adoption of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Several studies have since examined the progress made on various SDGs. One such focus has been on under-5 child mortality, both in national, regional, and global contexts (Liu et al., 2016 ). Investigations into newborn and under-5 child mortality in Africa and child mortality in China between 1996 and 2015 have been crucial in evaluating the significance of reaching the SDGs (Golding et al., 2017; He et al., 2017 ). Additionally, researchers have projected maternal mortality from 1990 to 2015 in the context of the SDGs (Alkema et al., 2016 ). Efforts to achieve the SDGs necessitate substantial resources to strengthen health systems in low-and middle-income countries (Stenberg et al., 2017 ). Authors like Fullman et al. (2017)  and Lozano et al. (2018)  further explored health-related advancements. Moyer and Bohl (2019)  examined policy frameworks' impact on SDGs implementation, while Pedercini et al. (2018)  highlighted the importance of simulation integrated models for SDGs strategy development. Salvia et al. (2019)  assessed the achievements from local and global perspectives, and Mohammed et al. (2016)  investigated the relationship between smart growth and meeting sustainability targets. Various aspects related to the accomplishment of the SDGs have been covered, including land degradation (Sims et al., 2019 ), sustainable well-being (Costanza et al., 2016 ), renewable energy (Schwerhoff and Sy, 2017 ), and life below the ocean (Singh et al., 2018 ). Allen et al. (2020)  evaluated Australia's progress toward achieving the SDGs using established methodologies. It's important to note that these research publications have focused on specific SDGs rather than all 17 of them. Considering the breadth of the SDGs, assessing progress toward their achievement can be viewed as a Multiple Criteria Decision-Making (MCDM) problem. While numerous MCDM approaches are available in the literature, selecting the best one can be challenging (Dağıstanlı, 2023; Kazemi et al., 2024 ). The choice often depends on the nature and complexity of the specific problem at hand. According to Stevic et al. (2019) , the results obtained through the MARCOS (Measurements Alternatives and Ranking according to COmpromise Solution) tool are more accurate than those from other popular MCDM techniques such as ARAS, Multi-Attributive Border approximation Area Comparison (MABAC), Weighted Aggregated Sum Product Assessment (WASPAS), Technique for Order Performance by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS), and others. On the other hand, Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA), known for its quantitative and analytical capabilities, is a highly suitable tool for evaluating the performance of multiple peer entities, often referred to as Decision-making Units (DMUs) or alternatives. This study is driven by the aim of determining the relative progress of each EU country towards achieving specific SDGs in comparison to others. This paper introduces several novel aspects that distinguish it from similar studies:• A unique DEA-MARCOS approach is devised to address decision-making challenges.  • An optimization model, utilizing maximum deviation measure and dispersion measure, is proposed to determine the weight values of criteria.  • The applicability of the DEA-MARCOS technique is demonstrated through a real-life case study that evaluates the progress of EU countries towards achieving SDGs.  • Furthermore, a comparative analysis is presented to highlight the superiority of the developed framework. Section 4  presents a real-life case study that demonstrates the application of the proposed methodology. The results and discussions are provided in Section 5 . The SDGs are an extension of the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs). The authors came to the conclusion that, by emphasizing the interactions between wealth, planet, partnership, and peace, the SDGs offer a far more comprehensive approach to sustainable development than the MDGs. Thus, with a focus on "political and institutional coherence," the SDGs are "indivisible and interconnected." The economic and ecological aspects of sustainable development now form the foundation of the SDGs. The MDGs' emphasis on reducing poverty has switched to integrated development, which includes reducing poverty. According to the literature analysis (Table 1 ), some research concentrates on the selected SDGs, while others examine trade-offs and synergies among all of the SDGs. SDGs 1 and 3 are typically closely tied to one another, however SDG 2 is a compromise goal. According to Pradhan et al. (2017) , the discovered synergies demonstrate the broad compatibility of the sustainable development objectives, wherein advancement towards one goal can benefit the attainment of other goals. Scholars employ a range of techniques to evaluate the extent of SDG implementation and ascertain their interrelationships, encompassing correlation, time series, multivariate data analysis, MCDM, and neural networks. An MCDM is a powerful tool for analyzing complex real-world issues due to its ability to compare multiple alternatives using predetermined criteria. These techniques consider various factors that define potential courses of action. Decision-makers assess these options based on established criteria and their intended decision-making objective. Over time, MCDM techniques have gained popularity in addressing a wide range of issues, and new methods have emerged (Wang et al., 2024 ). Allah Bukhsh et al. (2019)  classified MCDM approaches into three types: outranking methods, synthesis methods, and interactive methods. Outranking techniques allow decision-makers to rank options based on their preferences (Figueira et al., 2013 ). Synthesis methods prove useful when weighing frequently contradictory circumstances during decision-making (Simanaviciene et al., 2012 ). MCDM enables decision-makers to consider multiple criteria or goals, helping to resolve potential conflicts between different characteristics (Kumar et al., 2018 ). While numerous MCDM approaches are available in the literature, selecting the best one can be challenging. The choice often depends on the nature and complexity of the specific problem at hand. Stevic et al. (2019)  introduced the MARCOS approach for reasonable preference aggregation, involving defining the relationships between alternatives and ID/AID, imposing "ideal degree (ID)" and "anti-ideal degree (AID)" as reference points, and establishing the "utility degree (UD)" of each option with respect to ID and AID. According to (Stevic et al., 2019 ), the results obtained through the MARCOS method are more accurate than those from other popular MCDM techniques such as ARAS, MABAC, WASPAS, TOPSIS, and others. The MARCOS technique has found applications in various domains, including assessing human resources in the transport industry, selecting stackers in logistics systems, choosing suppliers, studying road traffic hazards, and ranking zero-emission last-mile delivery assessment. The DEA is an appropriate approach for assessing the performance of various peer entities, often known as DMUs or options. It provides momentous potential in handling with complex and occasionally unknown DMU behaviors, comprising complex connections on multiple inputs and outputs. It has wide applications in numerous situations and different scenarios has been established recently. For example, Andrejic and Kilibarda (2015)  utilized a collective PCA-DEA model to assess the effectiveness and choice of certain distribution channels. Fallahpour et al. (2016)  integrated a fuzzy MADM technique with DEA, genetic programming, and other tools to properly evaluate green suppliers. Fan et al. (2019)  successfully used the DEA strategy in the PFSs (Production Frontier Structures) environment to rank the GSS (Green Supplier Selection) problem, considering both subjective and quantitative criteria. Wu et al. (2019)  proposed an all-inclusive DEA technique to address the GSS problem within the context of interval-valued PFSs. Li et al. (2019)  developed a DEA model to measure the efficiency of the logistics sector in the Wuhan region of Central China. Yazdani et al. (2020)  introduced an integrated model combining DEA, FUCOM, and CoCoSo approaches based on rough set theory to select logistics centers in Spanish autonomous communities. Their strategy benefits from the use of aggressive, benevolent, and neutral cross-efficiency formulas to aid in selecting the ideal DMU among alternatives in a MCDM situation. They further combined the final cross efficiencies and created a comprehensive rating of the DMUs using the Ordered Weighted Averaging (OWA) method. Zhou and Zhan (2020)  suggested three DEA-based models to generate more realistic efficiency scores for DMUs, considering the desired ratings within the outputs and employing MCDM to calculate output weights. Pereira et al. (2021)  extended a state-of-the-art approach using DEA for evaluating the convergence of the low- and middle-income UN member states about SDG 6 between 2016 and 2017. (Pereira and Marques, 2021, 2022 ) presented a benchmarking procedure for assessing the performance of 123 low- and middle-income UN member states about water and sanitation services (WSS) development targets over the 2001–2015 period of Millennium Development Goals (MDGs). The applied framework to evaluate European education procedures is based on creating a composite indicator adopting a “benefit-of-the-doubt” model. Addressing negative data, Soltanifar and Sharafi (2022)  used a modified cross-efficiency method to rank DMUs in DEA. They proposed a fuzzy VIKOR variant for their suggested approach. In a hesitant fuzzy setting, Mishra et al. (2022)  employed a combination of DEA, FOCUM, and MABAC techniques to tackle sustainable supplier selection issues. Hasani and Mokhtari (2022)  developed a hybrid fuzzy DEA-MCDM approach. Oliviera et al. (2023)  integrated MCDM and cluster analysis method with DEA. Chou et al. (2023)  worked on the performance assessment of research and development organizations using hierarchical network DEA. For solving large scale DEA models, Muren et al. (2024)  developed a new parallel framework algorithm. Despite these diverse applications of DEA, none of the existing research has focused on suitable assessment of EU countries in terms of achieving SDGs (Bouraima et al., 2024 ). This research establishes the connection between the SDGs in EU countries. A unique DEA-MARCOS approach is the primary approach that permits us to analyze the complex phenomenon successfully. Two key research goals were set in the paper: i) Potential evaluation of European Union nations to evaluates the progress of EU countries towards achieving SDGs; ii) An optimization model, utilizing maximum deviation measure and dispersion measure, is proposed to determine the weight values of indicators SDGs (sensitivity analysis approach). We analyze seventeen SDGs and examine their linkages utilizing the 2020 Eurostat database in order to meet the research objectives. The innovative aspect of our research is based on the application of optimization techniques and sensitivity analysis (SA) to determine weights that yield the intended significance for each variable. The proposed DEA-MARCOS model has three parts described below. 3.1 Determination of efficient alternatives by Data envelopment analysis (DEA) Let there be m   alternatives to be assessed, each represented by various input and output attributes. Typically, non-beneficial attributes are designated as inputs, while beneficial attributes are considered as outputs. For each alternative, there are n   input attributes denoted by d  r l     ( r  =  1 , … , n )    and t   output attributes denoted by e  s l    ( s =  1 , … . . , t ) .    Let and represent the degrees of the r  th  input attribute and s th  output attribute for the l th  alternative, respectively. Austria is highlighted in the final results as the country that achieves the SDGs with the greatest amount of progress. An examination of the input data reveals that not every segment exhibits the highest level of success. In spite of this, Austria advances in a steady and equitable manner toward every assessed SDG. Austria and a few other countries have the greatest performance among all EU members in various areas, such as gross domestic expenditure on research and development. Austria has demonstrated significant progress towards the SDGs, with achievements consistently falling between medium and best, and consistently ranking among the nations with the best results across the SDGs. Mateusz et al. (2018)  employed the TOPSIS and VIKOR models with the primary objective of characterizing the progress made by the EU member states toward the SDGs. They discovered that, among some other EU countries, Austria, Belgium, Germany, Denmark, Finland, Italy, and Luxembourg are in first place, while Bulgaria and Romania are in lowest place. Piwowarski et al. (2018)  employed the TOPSIS and vector measure construction methodologies to analyze the changes in the EU's poverty region. Based on the aggregate results, Luxembourg, Finland, Austria, Malta, and Spain were shown to have the best results in terms of poverty reduction. When all is said and done, Romania and Bulgaria rank last once more. AHP technique was adopted by Martín and Carnero (2019)  to evaluate the attaining of the SDGs. According to the final results, Norway has a very high level of sustainability, while the Netherlands, Sweden, Denmark, and the United Kingdom all achieved excellent levels of sustainability. Romania and Bulgaria are once more on the very low list of countries in relation to the SDGs in this scenario. It is evident from looking at the results calculated by other researchers that there are similarities with the results covered in this study. It is evident that there are some similarities between the results pertaining to the top and bottom-ranked countries. Additionally, by evaluating each goal's development independently and the set of indicators linked to it, governments will be able to acquire a more accurate understanding of the current sustainability situation and improve their capacity to predict future trends. As a result of the suggested approach's success in the assessment of sustainability-related accomplishments, the direction for future work calls for conducting the assessment procedure based on MCDM approaches, adopted goals and indicators, and targeted values. By including the targeted values into the assessment model, it will be possible to obtain objective results that highlight any potential flaws that need to be fixed and better represent the existing situation and progress towards desired goals.   6 Conclusions This paper has offered a hybrid model by combining DEA, MARCOS, and an optimization model related to deviation and dispersion as a tool to make it easier to analyze how well the EU countries are doing in terms of reaching the SDGs. The MARCOS approach was used for the final ranking of the EU countries under consideration, and DEA was used to determine the most effective alternatives. A deviation and dispersion-based optimization model was also built for this purpose. The technique that has been established is based on 17 indicators that are related to the 17 SDGs. Although more indicators are introduced, the review procedure only uses 17 of them since they most accurately reflect the aims being considered. By doing so, the scenario of a complicated and broad model is avoided, as well as the indicator repetition. According to the final statistics, Austria has the best performance in terms of sustainable development. The conclusions reached in this way constitute a utility-based approach that takes into account the variations in the evaluation criteria. By doing this, it prevents a situation where some nations are ranked higher than others but only because they have made progress towards a small number of objectives. This workable compromise puts the balanced accomplishments of the evaluated nations into perspective. Additionally, the proposed method's effectiveness and dependability were confirmed by comparison with other established MCDM approaches. Even though some authors, like Miola and Schiltz (2019) , have claimed that the position of the country strongly depends on the chosen methodology and indicators, the inclusion of the SDG indicators in the appropriate decision model would help to provide a clear picture of the sustainability level achieved in the given country and their position relative to others. This article's primary weakness stems from the review process, which used data on just 17 indicators. All of the suggested indicators must be taken into account for the results to be more solid and trustworthy. Although DEA can handle multiple input and multiple output models, and DEA doesn’t require an assumption of a functional form relating inputs to outputs, it has some limitations: it is an extreme point technique, and statistical hypothesis tests are difficult for DEA because of its non-parametric form. Besides the weighing model presented in this study does not reflect the correlative relationships among the criteria. On the other hand, the proposed ranking algorithm eliminates the effects of different dimensions of criteria using only one normalization process, which may bias the acquired results as each normalization process may lose the original information slightly. Lastly, the developed method does not determine the dominance degree of each alternative to the other alternatives.
