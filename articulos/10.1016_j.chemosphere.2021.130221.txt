Galloway  Dear Editor Scientists, industry, and governments often collect valuable contaminant and nutrient data from wild marine fish, invertebrates, seaweed, and shellfish to support seafood safety, export and trade goals, and global food security efforts. Seafood also represents an important contaminant bridge between the atmosphere, hydrosphere (e.g., seawater quality), biota, and humans. As a result of growing globalization, marine-based foods are traded and consumed globally, creating a complex series of human exposure contaminant pathways (Huang et al., 2020 ). To evaluate these contaminant pathways, seafood datasets may be mined for use within the environmental governance and public health spheres especially in the context of marine policy relevant strategies in support of several of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (Lubchenco et al., 2020 ). Seafood safety datasets are often spatially explicit, carefully developed with accredited analytical chemistry methods, and include critical temporal trend information for biota that may be used in source-receptor models. These data can be linked to coastal and marine ecosystem databases to aid surveillance and monitoring efforts that support relevant multilateral environmental and chemical and hazardous waste agreements such as the Minamata, Basel, Rotterdam, and Stockholm Conventions. The UN-FAO and WHO (e.g., Codex Committee on Contaminants in Foods) also have led and organized several food security and global seafood safety databases and support a wide array of scientific capacity building efforts across the world. However, to date the use and policy applications of seafood safety data has been limited, due to several challenges, including legal obstacles, and the doctrines behind the European Union’s FAIR (EU-FAIR) data principles including findability, accessibility, interoperability and reusability (Wilkinson et al., 2016 ). Sustainable management of coastal and marine resources, and ocean health in general, has become of paramount importance to policy makers especially as we enter the UN Decade of Ocean Science for Sustainable Development (2021–2030) (Costello et al., 2020 ). The value of robust seafood safety datasets and using a global food security perspective, in general, to support multilateral environmental conventions has been previously underutilized (Bank et al., 2020 ). Furthermore, establishing linkages between the UN Decade of Ocean Science and the UN Decade of Nutrition (2016–2025) will likely have critical benefits to support their implementation by harnessing the collective infrastructure of both efforts. Regional and other global seafood safety datasets can support these programs, and together will provide a robust foundation for probability-based, human and ecological risk assessment models that can identify sensitive ecosystem areas, where biodiversity hotspots and, for example, mercury methylation and pollution hotspots overlap. Countries, individuals, and intergovernmental entities that maintain seafood safety databases can help support several of the relevant the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (e.g., SDG-2 Zero Hunger, SDG-3 Good Health and Well Being, SDG-13 Climate Action, and SDG-14 Life Below Water) by ensuring that these datasets are in compliance with the EU-FAIR data principles. Doing so can assist in validating environmentally realistic contaminant fate and transport models to support the appropriate effectiveness evaluation aspects of environmental conventions while also providing support for economic development, food security, and coastal community resilience (Hoegh-Guldberg et al., 2019 ).
