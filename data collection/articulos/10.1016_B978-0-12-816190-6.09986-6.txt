Introduction-Opportunities and Challenges in the Electronic Waste Management: Implementation of Innovations to Achieve Sustainable Development Goals Majeti Narasimha Vara Prasad  School of Life Sciences, University of Hyderabad, Hyderabad, India School of Life Sciences University of Hyderabad Hyderabad India    Research in the field of e-waste (waste electric and electronic equipment, WEEE) has been growing exponentially for the last two decades. This rapid progress in the field is inspiring researchers to create breakthrough applications. Hazardous substances management is a subject of importance for all nations owing to numerous concerns (see Fig. 1  ). In order to manage hazardous substances, the majority of nations worldwide have subscribed to, or are signatories, of the following international conventions: The Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movement of Hazardous Wastes and their Disposal. Website: http://www.basel.int/about.html . The Rotterdam Convention on the Prior Informed Consent (PIC) Procedure for Certain Hazardous Chemicals & Pesticides in International Trade. Website: http://www.pic.int/en/ViewPage.asp?id=104 . The Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs). Website: http://www.pops.int . The Minamata Convention on Mercury. Website: http://www.mercuryconvention.org/ . Strategic Approach to International Chemicals Management. Website: www.saicm.org . Overall, there has been a general feeling that the management of e-waste has opened a Pandora’s box of problems (Bakhiyi et al., 2018 ), in particular the magnitude of global e-waste and the hazards associated with its management (Baldé et al., 2017 ; Singh et al., 2018 ). Robust international/national policies and regulations are required to address these issues. Contaminants from e-waste flow into an ecosystem via different pathways, posing health risks to human and other biota (Wu et al., 2018 ). Therefore, numerous efforts for the reduction of e-waste and high-tech approaches such as robots in e-waste management are being researched (Alvarez-de-los-Mozos and Renteria, 2017 ) (Fig. 2  ). E-waste contains a large amount of technologically-relevant valuable trace elements in higher concentration than found in the range of naturally-available primary ores (Li, 2017 ). In a recent report from the New York Times  (July 5, 2018), the two sides of the coin of e-waste are exposed, mentioning that “it offers economic opportunity as well as toxicity”. However, it is also noted that “in 2016, the gold in the world’s e-waste equated to 1/10th of the gold mined globally that year.” Yet much of this treasure is simply reburied in landfills. Therefore, nations without natural resources are planning to recover such resources from e-waste, promoting global business-stimulating emerging economies, reverse logistics, and closed-loop supply chain for raw material for a sustainable circular economy (Friedrich, 2018 ; Islam and Huda, 2018 ; Tong et al., 2018 ). Diverse bacterial and fungi (many extremophiles) are the tools for the breakdown of the e-waste using different techniques. These aid in leaching technologically-important and precious metals for reuse degradation (Liu et al., 2018 ; Sahajwalla and Gaikwad, 2018 ). Notable examples include fungal biorecovery of gold from e-waste (Bindschedler et al., 2017 ) and computer-munching microbes: metal leaching from electronic scrap by bacteria and fungi (Brandl et al., 2001 ). In 2016, 44.7 metric tons (Mt) of e-waste was generated, out of which only 20% (8.9 Mt) was properly collected and recycled, while the rest was dumped as residual waste (Baldé et al., 2017 ). Thus, urban mining augments resource recovery (Tesfaye et al., 2017 ) (see Figs. 3–5    ). Metal recycling is possible for computer processing units (CPUs), mobile phones, connectors, circuit boards, scrap metals, printed circuit boards (PCBs), hard drives, memory, and other items, by the processes shown in Table 1  . E-waste management recycling consciousness is growing steadily (Fig. 6  ). The application of legislation for the collection and recycling of e-waste would contribute to UN and member states adopting a global agenda of sustainable development goals. According to Baldé et al. (2017) , about 4.8 billion people were covered by national legislation (= 66%, 67 countries). Developing and developed nations have evolved practices such as the characterization of valuable materials of WEEE; application of feasible technologies and practices for recovery of beneficial materials such as metals, precious metals, and rare earth elements; green design and resource recovery and remanufacturing; lifecycle assessment; and policy and standards for WEEE (Baldé et al., 2017 ). In 2016, the global quantity of e-waste was 44.7 Mt, which comprised small equipment (16.8 Mt), large equipment (9.1 Mt), heat exchange equipment (7.6 Mt), and screens (6.6 Mt). Lamps and small IT represented a smaller share of the global quantity of e-waste generated in 2016, 0.7 and 3.9 Mt, respectively. The e-waste estimate for 2021 will be about 55.2 Mt. The guesstimated annual growth rate is ~ 4%–5% (Baldé et al., 2017 ; Fig. 6 ).
