                  The Sustainable Development Goals or SDGs [44] are an ambitious step towards sustainable development, taking a much broader view of sustainability than ever achieved previously, yet practical challenges remain, including how to implement change. The aims of this research were to determine how an influential aquaculture company in Australia - Tassal, Tasmania's largest salmon aquaculture company - perceived the SDGs, and to ascertain the motivations and barriers for Tassal to work towards implementing the goals. Interviews were conducted with leaders, employees and external business partners. Tassal was not aware of the SDGs prior to this project, but were open to considering implementing them in their current sustainability practices. The survey responses were analysed using the Values-Rules-Knowledge (vrk) framework of decision making. Key findings were: 1) corporate and personal values were the key component driving Tassal's positive responses to the SDGs; 2) awareness of the SDGs resulted in Tassal recognising the potential gains from engaging with some of the seemingly less aquaculture-related goals (such as health and wellbeing). These findings demonstrate that businesses can fruitfully engage with the SDGs, even without government requirements or societal expectations, if they are prepared to broaden their interpretation of business sustainability and be reflective about their values. The vrk model is a potentially useful addition to current SDG and sustainability tools, such as those of the UN Global Compact, as a way to diagnose organisational barriers to adopting practices aligned with the SDGs. An emerging area of importance to social, economic and environmental sustainability – social license – was also identified as implicit in many, but not explicit in any of the SDGs.

CSIRO, Land and Water Hobart Australia       The Sustainable Development Goals or SDGs [44] are an ambitious step towards sustainable development, taking a much broader view of sustainability than ever achieved previously, yet practical challenges remain, including how to implement change. The aims of this research were to determine how an influential aquaculture company in Australia - Tassal, Tasmania's largest salmon aquaculture company - perceived the SDGs, and to ascertain the motivations and barriers for Tassal to work towards implementing the goals. Interviews were conducted with leaders, employees and external business partners. Tassal was not aware of the SDGs prior to this project, but were open to considering implementing them in their current sustainability practices. The survey responses were analysed using the Values-Rules-Knowledge (vrk) framework of decision making. Key findings were: 1) corporate and personal values were the key component driving Tassal's positive responses to the SDGs; 2) awareness of the SDGs resulted in Tassal recognising the potential gains from engaging with some of the seemingly less aquaculture-related goals (such as health and wellbeing). These findings demonstrate that businesses can fruitfully engage with the SDGs, even without government requirements or societal expectations, if they are prepared to broaden their interpretation of business sustainability and be reflective about their values. The vrk model is a potentially useful addition to current SDG and sustainability tools, such as those of the UN Global Compact, as a way to diagnose organisational barriers to adopting practices aligned with the SDGs. An emerging area of importance to social, economic and environmental sustainability – social license – was also identified as implicit in many, but not explicit in any of the SDGs.   Keywords Sustainable development goals  Aquaculture  Case study  Implementation  Social license    1 Introduction In 2015 the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) were released and ratified by the United Nations as part of Agenda 2030, an agreement by the United Nations to improve global sustainability by 2030 [42] . The SDGs are made up of 17 goals covering all aspects of sustainability and are an ambitious step toward actionable targets for sustainable development covering all aspects of sustainability and all sectors of society ( Fig. 1 ). Yet there remain a number of key challenges relating to implementation of the strategies and action plans required to achieve the goals. The main challenges include: coordinating local, national and global responses [22,4] , avoiding negative consequences from responses to goals (or parts there-of) in isolation [12,36–38] , accessing information and resources to understand the goals and how to respond [21,42] , and monitoring, evaluating and assessments of progress at all scales in particular sectors (e.g [3,17,39] .). Challenges to implementing the SDGs are particularly acute in Australia where the awareness of the SDGs amongst the government, the private sector and the public is generally low [41] . This may be because the Australian government and media generally focus on socio-economic and political issues over environmental or sustainability concerns [9 ]. For example, the last election saw ‘jobs and growth’ and ‘health and schools’ as the competing major policy areas for the two major parties. This is despite overwhelming evidence that social and economic outcomes depend on healthy environments [47, 8 ] especially in situations experienced in Australia where large-scale rapid climate changes are threatening the sustainability of existing cultural and economic activities based on natural ecosystems (e.g., tourism, fisheries, coastal development) causing damage and economic costs [24,7] . Additionally, Australian businesses seem to be less engaged with the SDGs than other countries in Europe, Africa and Asia, with only about half of businesses in Australia stating it was important for businesses to ‘sign up’ to the SDGs, compared to 90% in developing countries (PwC 2016). This is reflected in Australia being ranked only 20th on achievement of the SDGs overall [34] , needing to reduce carbon emissions per capita, obesity rates, nitrogen use and fisheries management of some industries to improve its status. The efforts in Australia relating to the SDGs are not all bad, however, with: (i) members of the Australian research community deeply engaged and increasingly calling on businesses to do the same (e.g [4,12,38,37,3,17] ); (ii) Australia participating in international efforts to maintain the spotlight on the SDGS (e.g. Future Earth [11] , ICSU, ISSC 2015 [20] ;); and (iii) some private industry and non-government groups beginning to mobilise in this area (see for example Sustainable Business Australia [27] , United Nations Business Action Hub [43] , WBCSD [45] , World Ocean Council and WWF) due to recognition of the opportunities created by the SDGs and growing pressures from global markets to support and align with the SDGs (e.g. SDG compass http://sdgcompass.org/ ). Case studies that demonstrate the successes, benefits, and lessons learned from businesses’ attempts to adopt new and adapt existing practices to achieve the SDGs, are a potentially influential way of promoting greater and more widespread awareness and proactive efforts across businesses and Governments in Australia (while also potentially supporting efforts more widely) in engaging with and contributing to the SDGs. This paper discusses the outcomes of introducing the SDGs to an Australian company and describing their responses, particularly from the perspective of the employees who will be tasked with integrating the SDGs into the everyday company processes. In so doing, the analysis focuses on an aquaculture company which aims to be highly progressive and truly sustainable, Tassal Pty Ltd (see below for details). Although lessons are derived from the experiences of a single firm in the aquaculture sector, their transferability to other firms, sectors and geographies is possible because of the wide ranging and systemic implications of the SDGs. In particular because the SDGs offer an integrated and systems approach to sustainability and so to viably achieve sustainability requires that any company will need to first reflect on and invest time and effort in understanding:1) the fundamental values (ethics) of their decision making and how these affect / drive or determine the culture, norms, and identity of the firm to ultimately provide the vision (direction) and motivation necessary for the sustained and difficult efforts required to achieve the SDGs; and  2) the systemic dimensions involved in achieving sustainability, where the inter-dependencies of the linked social-economic-environmental system are acknowledged and explicitly accounted for in decisions and where the barriers/constraints of the existing rules and the interests of incumbent players need to be tackled.    Consequently the lessons identified here have broad meaning and relevance to existing Corporate Social Responsibility protocols and certification schemes by requiring more comprehensive initiatives targeting systemic barriers to sustainability. This parallels increasing public demands for greater transparency in certifications and broader measures for business accountability. The analysis therefore adopted a diagnostic and analytical model called the values-rules-knowledge (vrk) approach to diagnosing and assessing the systemic barriers and opportunities faced by a firm when considering innovative practices for sustainability. The vrk model highlights and provides a structured approach to understand the complex interactions between the systems of values, rules and knowledge that enable and constrain what businesses can and cannot do in regards to adopting novel ideas and options such as the SDGs. Detailed contextual examination and deliberation such as this is essential to ensure the participating company gains valuable insights into its personnel and operations and to reveal ideas for how the company could best implement the SDGs. The discussion in this paper is local and contextual but is still relevant globally to drive broader implementation through discussion of the practical steps of engaging with the SDGs.  2 The case study company: Tassal Pty Ltd Based in Australia's southern island, Tasmania, Tassal is Australia's largest producer of fresh salmon products, producing around 45,000 t of Atlantic salmon per annum at a farm gate value of $500 million. It is a vertically integrated company that includes freshwater hatcheries, saltwater aquaculture, primary processing, and value adding stages through to distribution, wholesaling and export. The overarching strategic focus for Tassal is “to deliver sustainable long term returns to shareholders as the leader in salmon in Australia, selling a highly recognised ethical valued brand and product to Australian consumers and retailers” [32] . Their strategic plan focuses on being a global leader in environmental, operational and financial returns. Goal 14 of the Sustainable Development Goals: “Conserve and sustainably use the oceans, seas and marine resources for sustainable development” [44]  is a particularly significant area, as 70% of the world is covered in water. In Australia the marine environment is important for employment and the economy, local food production and exports, tourism, transport, culture and recreation – and an important part of the Australian identity. Despite potentially having some of the best managed fisheries in the world [19] , Australia ranked particularly low in Goal 14 of the SDGs overall [34] . This low result might be because of a lack of information as there are “significant gaps in terms of basic knowledge of the current state of the oceans, seas and marine resources and how they interact with other sectors” ([36] , p34). Nevertheless it demonstrates the SDGs in the marine environment require urgent attention in Australia and globally. Global demand for seafood is increasing and there are substantial drivers of change affecting marine species and ecosystems, potentially compromising their ability to continue to be productive [8 ]. According to the Global Salmon Initiative [13] , wild caught fisheries will be significantly depleted beyond 2050. With the world population expected to reach 10 billion by 2050, food security will become a serious issue for large proportions of the global population [8 ]. Aquaculture continues to grow around the world and the FAO projects that by 2025 over half of all seafood consumed globally will be farmed [8 ]. In Australia, most (64%) seafood production is still dominated by wild catch [25] . Aquaculture also adds to the demand for wild catch, sourcing feed for carnivorous fish [35] . Even with a growing aquaculture industry – salmon aquaculture in particular – Australia still imports more seafood than it exports, and this gap is increasing [25] . In addition to a rising global population, growing incomes and demand for fish products, conflicts are emerging from new (and expanding) uses of marine environments such as across recreational and commercial fisheries, conservation groups, tourism and mining [2,44] . These growing demands are now widely recognised to be putting unsustainable pressure on marine environments and threatening the ‘social license to operate’ of many firms and industries in the marine sector [6,23] . Exacerbating these challenges are the growing changes to ocean temperatures, acidity and currents caused by climate change [18] , which can negatively affect fish health and growth rates (including salmon in farms) and cause substantial shifts in suitable habitats. Tassal is a useful case study because of their commitment to sustainability. They were the first aquaculture company globally to have all of their marine operations certified by the Aquaculture Stewardship Council [1] . Another unique feature is that Tassal produces independently reviewed annual sustainability reports [28–33]  to publicly document their practices. With recent expansion into a new lease site causing some conflict in terms of different values for different stakeholders concerning the same resource, such as recreational, cultural, commercial and ecological values, Tassal are also interested in how they can better understand and engage with these diverse stakeholders and demonstrate their commitment to sustainability.  3 Conceptual and analytical framework The values-rules-knowledge (vrk ) model of decision making (by individuals, groups, communities, or organisations) has been shown to be an effective framework for analysing and diagnosing the institutional (formal and informal), values-based (culture, norms, preferences, expectations, identity, motivations) and informational (knowledge) factors that constrain or enable decisions [16] . The model proposes that the behaviours, options, roles, responsibilities, or actions that a decision maker is legally, legitimately, and credibly allowed to adopt are determined by the prevailing systems of societal values, rules and knowledge, and their interactions ( Fig. 2 ). In practical terms, the vrk model (Fig. 2 ) provides an analytical framing and procedure to help identify and interrogate the context of decisions (choices) and behaviours (actions) in terms of the degree to which:• the relevant actors know the drivers and outcomes of changes in their socio-economic and natural environments and the consequences of interventions implemented in response (knowledge )  • the relevant actors want the outcomes from the changes and responses (i.e., are these outcomes desirable, and for whom) (values )  • the outcomes of the changes, and actors responses, are legally allowed and accord with social or cultural norms (formal and informal rules )    Examples of key aspects and interactions of values (v), rules (r) and knowledge (k) which are co-evolving and generally mutually reinforcing, are summarised in  Table 1 .  4 Research method and engagement strategy Given the above noted dearth of qualitative accounts of the SDGs in practice, the aim of this work was to examine how a large and leading business in its sector was engaging with the SDGs to extract lessons for the broader research and policy community about motivations and barriers to implementation. The research method and engagement strategy was based upon the newly developed vrk model of decision-making described above (Fig. 2  and Table 1 ) and the recently proposed SDG compass (see  Fig. 3 ) which has been developed to help organisations understand and integrate the SDGs into their practices (GRI, UN Global Compact and WBCSD 2015). This paper, therefore, focuses on presenting the key findings of our review and interviews that start to answer key questions about the theory and practice of the SDGs and sustainability decision making particularly reflecting on the theory and practice of vrk model. The research method comprised two main activities: 1) a desk top review of the relevant documentation from Tassal, including industry and organisational reports and operating manuals, news articles, and current assessments such as Global Reporting Index [14 ] and sustainability reports, and 2) face to face interviews with relevant and influential Tassal employees. Since the two research activities draw upon and are guided by the vrk perspective on the decision context and the SDG compass, this work acts as a test of methods that are potentially more widely applicable ( Table 2 ). After an initial approach to explore Tassal's interest in participating, three members of the environment and sustainability team at Tassal (which has seven staff members) volunteered to be part of the project. Three meetings were held to read through the full suite of SDGs and design the scope of the project. At the first meeting the team were introduced to the SDGs and each goal was discussed in turn. Over the following weeks while the team familiarised themselves with the goals and reflected on them, documentation relating to Tassal (reports, news articles, operations, current assessments) was collated. A second meeting was held to choose specific goals relevant to Tassal. Of the 17 goals, three were chosen that represented core lines of business that Tassal already aimed to deliver on (Goal 2, 8 and 14) and three that represented more challenging or future lines of business (Goal 3, 4, 9). The description of these six goals, including their sub-components, were then collaboratively ‘mapped’ to the existing Tassal vision, goals, organisational objectives and activities as documented in reports and policies. The mapping involved listing Tassal practices next to the SDG target description that it achieved. If there was no match, the SDG target was left blank. This part of the desk-top study informed the subsequent findings about where there might be synergies, gaps or trade-offs between the SDGs and the existing goals and practices of Tassal to inform their strategy moving forward. The output from this analysis is presented in  Table 3 . As a desk-top analysis, evaluating the effectiveness of the policies implemented by Tassal is out of scope. Third party assessments were used where available, such as Marine Stewardship Council. Over the next few weeks, interview questions and project descriptions were drafted and ethical procedures completed (approval number 94/15). A third meeting verified the questions and identified staff members who were subsequently approached by the environment and sustainability team for an interview. A small sample was deemed appropriate to gauge initial responses, interest and ideas and so high-level representatives of key business areas were targeted for interview (e.g. section leaders and key partners in the business such as suppliers and sustainability advisors). 11 interviews were held from May to July 2016, usually for 40 min to an hour and were audio-recorded. During and following each interview comprehensive notes were written up. Respondents were not asked to separate their thoughts on values, knowledge and rules, as these are often interlinked and it was important to avoid leading or constraining participants’ responses by forcing their ideas into these categories. The vrk model was not explicitly introduced to respondents for this reason as well, and because the SDGs themselves are already quite complicated. The vrk model was used to analyse the results because it highlights the different components shaping decisions. The data were interrogated for evidence of knowledge, values and rules interactions as drivers of decisions, perceptions and outcomes.  5 Results and discussion The headline findings from the results are that it is the values of sustainability and stewardship espoused by the organisation and its employees that are the main factors driving their engagement with the SDGs and that explain the existing alignments of their culture and practices with some of the SDGs. Organisational knowledge about the SDGs and government rules (policies and regulations) associated with the SDGs have played minor roles in influencing Tassal's engagement with the SDGs. This is because of the generally low availability and accessibility of information about the SDGs in Australia and because there are few official or formal policies or regulations relating to the SDGs in Australia. The second headline finding is that the systems of rules and knowledge of Tassal and those of its employees are showing signs of being highly responsive (changeable) as understanding and awareness of the SDGs grows. This is highlighted in the speed at which demands for knowledge products (tools, methods, guidelines) about the SDGs and activities exploring possible reforms to existing organisational rules (practices, procedures) in support of the SDGs have emerged in the organisation. Importantly, this responsiveness of the rules and knowledge of the employees and organisation to the SDGs can be largely explained by the alignment of the core values between the SDGs, Tassal and the employees. The third headline finding is that there is an important need and rapidly growing demand for the R&D community to be ready and able to support industries with relevant knowledge, tools and processes that can rapidly enable actions in pursuit of the SDGs. And this is especially the case for the GRI, UN Global Compact and WBCSD (2015) set of tools which seem to be largely focused on helping businesses assess the performance of their practices and procedures and policies against SDG indicators but fall short of helping companies diagnose the proximate and systemic causes of poor performance or diagnose barriers/constraints preventing a company from being able to change its policies and practices. Introducing and integrating the vrk perspective (and associated guidance on how to diagnose the systemic causes of problems and reveal strategic interventions to overcome these) into existing UN Global Compact tools and the SDG compass could be an extremely valuable contribution to the existing efforts of the UN Global Compact. Simply put, the vrk model helps businesses to understand the SDGs in ways that are appropriate to them, and highlight the barriers and interventions of changes that may be required. The remainder of this section presents more detailed explorations of the vrk interactions and dynamics caused by raising the awareness and understanding of the Tassal employees of the SDGs and the systemic issues associated with undertaking the necessary changes required to achieve the SDGs. The sections discuss the kr, vr and vk interactions separately even though the vrk model and perspective highlights and emphasises that all decisions, choices and actions are determined by the interactions between all of them. The reason they are dealt with separately is because doing so allows the complexities of reality to be meaningfully reduced in order to reveal useful findings from which important and transferable lessons can be identified. 5.1 Knowledge-rules interactions This section discusses the interactions of knowledge and rules, such as the science-policy interface and governance arrangements. Only four of the interviewees had heard of the SDGs before the project and all of these were external to Tassal (suppliers, advisors). This lack of awareness at the time does not mean that the non-governmental (e.g. WWF, Global Compact, Sustainable Business Australia) efforts over the last 6 months or so to raise the profile of the SDGs in Australia were unsuccessful, but rather that the timing of this project was before these campaigns had begun to show effect. It is acknowledged that Tassal would have become aware of the SDGs in the absence of this project, because it was already working with the WWF on other matters, but this project clearly hastened their awareness. All of the interviewees commented that the SDGs were wide in scope and scale and that this made them difficult to understand. At the same time, however, this highlighting of the relevance to the company of larger scales, higher levels of decision making, and wider sets of entities/actors also provided inspiration to begin looking and collaborating beyond oneself; thus emphasising the important role that knowledge can play in catalysing new relationships, norms and practices (rules). Yet, each interviewee noted that just being able to comprehend the full extent of SDGs in the first instance was a barrier to implementation. When asked to think about how to implement the SDGs, interviewees expressed difficulties in distinguishing between their personal roles as global citizens and those as company employees. Most interviewees saw that their roles as citizens and employees were intertwined, so working to achieve business sustainability was also related to working within the community, or at home, to be more sustainable. Again, the grand scale and scope of the SDGs had conflicting effects on many of those surveyed; inspiring many to take up the challenge of improving sustainability in all aspects of their lives, while also being overwhelming and partially or temporarily demotivating.“It's hard for people to see how they fit into the global picture of the SDGs” (Interview 8).   “[The SDGs] are a gargantuan task, a big hairy audacious goal it would require everyone on earth to work together” (Interview 7).   “The big downside is it's overwhelming” (Interview 9).   “The more small players you get singing from the song book the better” (Interview 4).    This mixture of inspiration and demotivation is matched in other business and government activities occurring in this area. For example, some groups are mobilising, such as the Sustainable Business Australia industry group which hosted a conference in 2016 on the SDGs, and large companies such as BHP, Coco-Cola, and Unilever are engaging early with the SDGs and incorporating them into their monitoring and reporting (see [26] ). But many smaller companies are still grappling with understanding and translating into practice the sheer number, breadth, inter-dependencies and implications of the SDGs (PwC 2016); which might be because of a lack of capacity (time or money) to invest in building this understanding and then acting on the implications. The SDGs are voluntary and there is no standard for reporting or auditing in place and no real clarity or guidance on the appropriate governance arrangements and core capabilities required to adopt and implement the SDGs at different scales of decision making. This ‘ambiguity’ or opaqueness in the practices and capabilities required to achieve the SDGs is a barrier to making progress because of the inertia it causes in stakeholders engaging with and adopting the SDGs. However, the ambiguity also provides opportunities because of the openness and flexibility it provides for organisation to creatively and adaptively develop and learn new ways of doing things that contribute to sustainability [10,46,5] .   6 Values-rules interactions This section explores the values and rules interactions of cultural practices and norms and the formal objectives, policies and structures of the organisation. All of the interviewees expressed support for Tassal to engage with the SDGs and to try to lead the way in business sustainability in their sector. A main motivation expressed was for Tassal to be progressive and innovative and to be seen as an industry leader. These aspirations and motivations were based on the interviewees expressed personal and corporate values to be progressive and sustainable. There was a range of views, however, on whether Tassal could achieve these lofty aims, with many raising Tassal's relatively small size and its more local to regional focus and scale as potential issues and concerns.“[Tassal] is incredibly progressive, really innovative, and proactive, and isn’t waiting for the rest of the industry to come with it. It is definitely leading by example ” (Interview 2).  “I see Tassal being a really important player in the SDGs. You can link aquaculture to pretty much all of the goals. I think the aquaculture industry will play a major role and Tassal being a world leader can spearhead that. Over 50% of the fish consumed around the world now is from aquaculture. Wild caught fisheries are in decline, so fish farming can play a really important role… Individuals and individual businesses drive change. I haven’t ever seen change happen so quickly as a business seeing a commercial rationale for change. Tassal share price went up 4% when they announced they were ASC certified. It's leading, forward thinking organisations that are going to drive sustainability forward”  (Interview 6).  “We have pushed the envelope internationally around feed substitution, so the chicken and vegetable meal substitution of fish meal and fish oil, you still have to have some fish oil, but developing the rendering plant, that was another way of doing it, instead of composting fish guts we are actually turning it into fish meal and oil which can be used for other stock feeds. So we built those two things in which are two ideas we had which could fit in with food security ” (Interview 1).  “Tassal are experimenting with different ratios of wild caught fish which they have got up to 90% fish free. It is not yet possible to get to 100% because you need the long chain fatty acids for fish growth and also human health ” (Interview 8).   Despite low knowledge of the SDGs within Tassal, it was apparent from analysis of their documents that many of Tassal's existing organisational objectives and activities align fairly readily with numerous SDG goals and target actions. Perhaps not surprisingly, most of the SDG-relevant business policies, strategies and/or procedures in Tassal closely aligned with the specific targets of Goal 14 (“Conserve and sustainably use the oceans, seas and marine resources for sustainable development”). The challenges we see are quite aligned with global challenges. But then the other thing I thought was we are actually quite on the ball, we are actually doing some of it”  (Interview 1).  “We think about these things regardless, because it creates a sustainable business. I look through these goals in number 14 and all of these things we talk about on a regular basis ” (Interview 4).   A surprising finding of the interviews was that many respondents related to a number of different goals and particularly related Tassal's mission to Goal 3 of the SDGs (i.e., to ‘Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages’). This was surprising because Goal 3 was not identified by the environment team at the outset of the project as being particularly relevant or a priority, and demonstrates that Tassal may gain from engaging with the SDGs outside the most obvious choices. Interviewees connection to Goal 3 may be because of Tassal's values-based business focus being to develop and sustain corporate culture of health and safety and of striving to get everything right.“With the recent expansion into DeCosti[partner company] there is an increased need to make sure that the culture continues, with good communication etc., with slightly shifting priorities ” (Interview 11).  “Our health and safety journey over the last five years has been to go from a place where we had a lot of incidences on an annual basis, we had to fix a lot of infrastructure, behaviour issues…Look at the transformation in five years, it's visible, it's dramatic…The overarching attitude [now] is ‘I care for and I take care’. A caring approach ” (Interview 7).  “I’ve changed from just worrying about my own patch to thinking about the business as a whole…the culture has changed in the last five years from a more siloed business to a more integrated one, where education is valued, as is helping each other and taking responsibility ” (Interview 5).  “There has been a culture change in the team (and the business) relating to not just doing the bare minimum but really striving for the best first time result ” (Interview 11).  “Zero harm to fish is a framework that was developed piggy backing off our work in safety, which is our zero harm to people. That is the goal, to ensure that everyone in the workplace has adequate health and wellbeing and does not come to harm in the workplace. It's the same for the fish. Essentially, we want to drive the culture of stockmanship, which means a conscientious farming effort, you have farmers, you have stockmen, that understand their animals and understand their behaviour so they know what is adversely affecting well-being for example, and what their operations are doing … optimising fish health and welfare will eventually mean you have an increase in profits because if the animal has better wellbeing the animal is in an environment that will optimise growth and feed conversion, so that's our philosophy ” (Interview 4).   Finally, interviewees also indicated that Tassal could take a leading role, in conjunction with industry groups and state and local government, to drive some policy and regulatory developments and reforms that incentivise translation of the SDGs into more locally relevant policies (including standards, guidelines, and policies around engagement). It is important for reasons relating to alignment with their core values, objectives, and culture that Tassal make sure their practices are aligned with societal (local) community values and that this is widely and effectively communicated; the SDGs provide a potentially helpful framework to realise this.“The SDGs is a framework to help organisations focus on things that are important to people, stakeholders and the planet and for organisations like Tassal to take a leadership position. From my experience it's far more effective to transform an industry one company at a time.”  (Interview 6).    7 Knowledge-values interactions This section explores the knowledge-values interactions such as identity, roles, expectations and perceptions. The project highlighted that the potential for Tassal to work towards implementing the SDGs was high, with the range of benefits likely to be achieved including potentially helping to overcome current challenges to their social license to operate in new sites and to further build the culture of the company internally and externally.“We want to improve the social side through participation, everything we have achieved has been with collaboration” (Interview 1).    Although there was consensus support for engagement with the SDGs, the level of confidence in the likely success or the scale of the leadership role that Tassal should take varied. There was uncertainty about the effectiveness of explicitly implementing the SDGs and about their effectiveness as a communication and marketing tool. Some felt the public would respond positively, while others felt the SDGs were too complex and confusing.“People have had it with certifications so the SDGs is potentially a fresh way to engage that is more meaningful to consumers ” (Interview 1).  “I don’t expect that most consumers are going to pay a lot of attention to the SDGs, a small minority of the public will have heard of them ” (Interview 9).   In addition, Tassal's role compared with government and public actions was debated. Some felt that Tassal could play a part in driving the SDGs even on an international scale, others felt that the business was too small to play a leadership role and that the main drivers for the SDGs needed to be public (values) and government (rules and regulations) led. The interviews found that Tassal might have the most to gain from further learning about goals outside of Goal 14, but participants also identified that they could play a role in educating others, or helping set standards for aspects of Goal 14. The most cited example of this was their ASC certification which could act as a “great standard for the SDGs” (Interview 6). Another example where Tassal could help develop a benchmark is in the exploration and instantiation into clear protocols of Integrated Multi-trophic Aquaculture (such as urchin ranching, nutrient recovery and reuse) and environmental offsets such as kelp farming.“We try to reduce the nitrogen, trying to understand the assimilative capabilities of the water is as important as reducing it…We have taken a lead role in industry recovering nutrients from the hatchery rather than releasing ” (Interview 1).   A further potential part of Tassal engaging with the SDGs into the future includes contributing to salmon aquaculture on a global scale – through sharing knowledge of current best practices or sharing technical skills and expertise. This kind of international collaboration is essential in achieving the overarching aim of the SDGs – global sustainability.“We have influenced things internationally … I know we get watched, the corporate social responsibility reporting area is quite competitive … we have a verbal commitment to work on some third world technology transfer ” (Interview 1).    8 Conclusion For businesses to operate sustainably they need to be economic and environmentally viable and socially acceptable. The SDGs cover all of these broad issues and present a comprehensive set of goals, targets and actions against which a company can develop its sustainability and self-evaluation strategies. Yet the SDGs remain unproven in practice in the private sector, which is largely explained by: the differences in the language used in the SDGs compared with that of business; few successful examples of where the SDGs have been successfully adopted, largely because of the difficult and complex systemic changes required of the organisation and society more broadly in order to do so; and few regulatory mechanisms incentivising their adoption. All of these factors provide immediate constraints or barriers slowing the translation and adoption of the SDGs into business. The interviews with section leaders and key partners of the Tassal aquaculture company in Australia found that although most employees had little knowledge of the SDGs prior to the project, perceptions of the SDGs were favourable and there was a high level of support for the business to continue to work towards implementing the SDGs. The interviews also identified that, in the absence of the requisite knowledge and any official rules requiring or incentivising the adoption of the SDGs, it was the values of the organisation and of the employees that were the key determinants of the positive perceptions and willingness to adopt the SDGs. In other words, it was the alignment of values between individuals, the business and those perceived to be represented by the SDGs that provided the strongest motivator for Tassal to engage with and attempt to implement changes and actions in pursuit of the SDGs. As a private company, Tassal was also motivated to improve its competitive edge, comparative advantage, economic performance, social license to operate, and the public perceptions of the industry as a whole. It is noteworthy that although the SDGs are not explicitly about social license, there is a clear need and potential benefit to further explore how the two are related and interact, both within Tassal and generally. Barriers for Tassal in engaging with the SDGs include the complexity, scope and scale of the goals compared with the medium and regional nature of the business. Tassal felt happy to try to take a lead in this area, but uncertainty remained about how consumers might respond. More real world case discussions of practical applications of the SDGs are still required to better understand how they can begin to be applied and to achieve the broader uptake that is necessary to achieve the ambitious targets of the SDGs – geographically, institutionally and culturally – and more discussion is required to facilitate cross-learnings and innovative approaches (across sectors and scales). Furthermore, new assessment mechanisms involved in the SDGs that directly link desirable SDG outcomes with the necessary systemic organisational changes are still a much needed area of further research. In particular, greater recognition for the crucial influence of the context of values, rules and knowledge that shape the available decisions is needed. The values-rules-knowledge model was identified as a potentially powerful approach that could start to address this need. Applying the SDGs is an opportunity to examine business practices from a different perspective, at a higher level, and requires reflection on core business responsibilities and values. This can be a positive process and open up areas for subtle, or even substantial changes. The cycle of reflection and change is one that offers multiple benefits for businesses and individuals by making sure that core values, objectives and practices remain on track with the broader picture of the world and where it is headed in sustainable development terms. Engaging with the SDGs, especially in the context of the vrk model, helps identify a broader suite of actions to achieve greater business sustainability. The environment and sustainability team at Tassal will continue to look into how the business can monitor and report on aspects of the SDGs and how to engage with the SDGs more broadly across scales of local and international business, government and public communities. Lessons for other Australian and international businesses to take from this account include: engaging with the SDGs is not easy but is likely to be essential. The SDGs are a much broader view of sustainability than currently exists in any certification or sustainability programs. It requires reflection on the values, rules and knowledge systems that are currently used to develop mission statements, action plans and reports and for all other business tools.
