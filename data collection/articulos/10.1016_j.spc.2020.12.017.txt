                  There need to be continual improvements in the research methods used in cooking studies to benefit the ecological environment and human health. The literature must be systematically reviewed to facilitate the discovery, optimization, and rational design of cooking studies in the context of sustainable development goals. Thus, this paper raises several important design considerations for cooking studies that should be implemented in future measurements. Herein, 131 scientific articles were selected and analyzed according to four aspects: (1) influential factors, (2) cooking emissions, (3) health effects, and (4) source apportionment. The analysis led to the identification of representative test methods and a perspective on the future of research in the identified aspects.

Review article  Insights into cooking sources in the context of sustainable development goals Jing Li  School of Environment and Energy, South China University of Technology, Guangzhou Higher Education Mega Center, Guangzhou 510006, China School of Environment and Energy South China University of Technology Guangzhou Higher Education Mega Center Guangzhou 510006 China  School of Environment and Energy, South China University of Technology, Guangzhou Higher Education Mega Center, Guangzhou 510006, China      Editor: Prof. Adisa Azapagic There need to be continual improvements in the research methods used in cooking studies to benefit the ecological environment and human health. The literature must be systematically reviewed to facilitate the discovery, optimization, and rational design of cooking studies in the context of sustainable development goals. Thus, this paper raises several important design considerations for cooking studies that should be implemented in future measurements. Herein, 131 scientific articles were selected and analyzed according to four aspects: (1) influential factors, (2) cooking emissions, (3) health effects, and (4) source apportionment. The analysis led to the identification of representative test methods and a perspective on the future of research in the identified aspects.   Graphical abstract Image, graphical abstract       Keywords Influential factors  Residential kitchens  Commercial kitchens  Emission factors  Health Effects  Source Apportionment    1 Introduction The process of cooking can emit different size particles including over a hundred of chemical species (AbdullahiÂ etÂ al., 2013 ; HuangÂ etÂ al., 2011 ; KabirÂ and Kim,Â 2011 ; SeeÂ and Balasubramanian,Â 2006 ), to produce fumes and odors that occur both in residential and commercial kitchens (WuÂ etÂ al., 2012b ). A continuous indoor emissions conducted for a prolonged period after the end of cooking significantly decreases their levels, which mostly contribute to the formation of outdoor organic aerosols (OAs) (RobinsonÂ etÂ al., 2018 ). The (PM)2.5 -bound polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon (PAHs), trace elements, and gaseous aldehydes are primary pollutants with cancerogenic or tumor promoting characteristics (LiÂ etÂ al., 2014 ; WuÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). In addition, they increase the risk of various diseases for restaurant workers and the women engaged in long-term cooking activities (SeeÂ and Balasubramanian,Â 2006 ). Studies of influential factors generally consider the impacts of cooking methods (ZhaoÂ etÂ al., 2019 ), oils and their temperatures (ZhaoÂ etÂ al., 2018 ), spices (LiuÂ etÂ al., 2017 ), fuels (HuangÂ etÂ al., 2011 ), pan and burner types (AmoueiÂ Torkmahalleh etÂ al., 2018 ; BroomandiÂ etÂ al., 2020 ), and attached range hoods (SingerÂ etÂ al., 2017 ) during representative testing. A review of the relationships between emission rates, particle concentrations and size distributions, and influential factors has been published previously (AmoueiÂ Torkmahalleh etÂ al., 2017 ); it did not take into account odor-emitting chemicals. Furthermore, the pollutants emitted during Chinese-style cooking such as particles, PAHs, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), carbon oxides (COx ), and nitrogen oxide (NOx ) have been quantified and analyzed in terms of influential factors (ZhaoÂ and Zhao,Â 2018 ). In mainland China, emissions of particles, VOCs, carbonyl compounds, PAHs, COx , sulfur dioxide (SO2 ), and NOx  and their effects on human health were determined (WangÂ etÂ al., 2017b ). It is also necessary to discuss emissions for different cooking styles and areas. The global emissions and concentrations of the volume-based particles contained in chemical profiles and source apportionments were reviewed in 2013 (AbdullahiÂ etÂ al., 2013 ); however, the utilized apportionment methods have been significantly modified since that time, owing to the recent developments of numerical simulation techniques. And, more remarkable was that the relation between the exposure to cooking oil fumes and the risk of lung cancer could not be expressed superficially by mechanisms or declaration form (LeeÂ and Gany,Â 2013 ; WangÂ etÂ al., 2017a ). The effects of fuels, cooking methods, and ingredients on emissions, emission inventories for domestic and commercial cooking activities, human health and environment, regulations, and guidelines were summarized in 2011 by considering the global processes (KimÂ etÂ al., 2011 ). However, a short summary describing the results of recent studies is required. It should (1) discuss how to quantify emissions depending on various influential factors for different cooking styles; (2) perform emission quantification for residential and commercial kitchens; (3) outline the effects of various emissions on human health; and (4) develop models for cooking source apportionments. After clarifying these issues, clear directions describing the desirable goals and future cooking source studies must be provided. The objective of this review was to accomplish these tasks in the context of sustainable development goals (SDGs) (HanÂ etÂ al., 2019 ; QuansahÂ etÂ al., 2017 ).  2 Methods 2.1 Cooking source vs SDGs In September 2015, the United Nations General Assembly adopted a resolution identifying 17 sustainable development goals (SDGs), with 169 related targets to be achieved by 2030. The resolution was approved by all representative countries. Some of the goals exhibit a strong relationship with cooking emissions (Fig.Â 1  ) as cooking sources lead to air pollution. Thus, insight into studies on cooking sources is necessary in the context of sustainable development goals. This review aims to organize, discuss, and analyze specific cooking source studies, and the methods of these studies, to direct possible future investigations and to achieve sustainable development goals.  2.2 Systematic literature review design The scrutiny of scientific literature is dependent on Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines (BressanelliÂ etÂ al., 2020 ; MoherÂ etÂ al., 2009 ). A four-step process following PRISMA guidelines was used to ensure a highly representative selection of studies (Fig.Â 2  ). 2.2.1 Identification To identify relevant studies, two sets of keywords were employed: (1) cooking: methods, oils, spices, pan, stove fuels, ventilation, emission factors, health effects, and (2) cooking source apportionment. The search in Environmental Sciences of Scopus led to the identification of 5315 Research/Review articles from 2005 to 2020. Additionally, Google Scholar was used to complement the initial dataset, leading to the inclusion of 30 additional papers.  2.2.2 Screening A total of 3577 articles remained after excluding duplicates. The quality and relevance of scientific articles was evaluated depending on their publication in journals with an impact factor as per Clarivate Analytics; 1773 articles were excluded in this screening step. A further screening step for the remaining 1804 articlesâreading the title and abstractâ excluded 1552 articles.  2.2.3 Eligibility and inclusion Three eligibility criteria were defined: (1) exploring influential factors of cooking methods, oil, spices, pans, stove fuels, and ventilation; (2) measuring cooking emissions to evaluate emission factors or health effects; and (3) clarifying source apportionment of anthropogenic sources, including cooking sources. The conclusions drawn from this step excluded 121 articles and led to a final considered set of 131 papers.    3 Results and discussion 3.1 Influential factors Multiple articles have aim at analyzing and quantifying relationships between emissions and influential factors (Fig.Â 3   â5  ). However, few articles have designed practical assessments for directly evaluating influential factors. Thus, the practical value of most articles is insufficient, and an optimal cooking process, with emissions and related impacts, has not been developed widely so far. Fig.Â 3  shows the division of articles with respect to the cooking method used, in which cooking tofu was the primary test method for Chinese style cooking (SeeÂ and Balasubramanian,Â 2008 ; WuÂ etÂ al., 2012a ). These measurements provided concentrations of all particle-bound chemicals, emission rates, and ultrafine particle (UFP) size distributions. Later, a more complicated test was conducted in one study on cooking potatoes and pork loin (PengÂ etÂ al., 2017 ), higher concentrations of aldehydes coincident with increased unsaturated fatty acids in oils were associated with specific cooking methods (e.g., deep-frying). Two representative articles adopted Chinese-style meals, all of which combined quantitative and qualitative methods for (PM)2.5  (LuÂ etÂ al., 2019 ), VOCs, and particle-bound chemicals (ZhaoÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). Similarly, cooking common dishes and pasta in a Western style represented real-world cooking activities, thereby improving the study design (O'LearyÂ etÂ al., 2019 ) compared to studies where only a single food was cooked (AmoueiÂ Torkmahalleh etÂ al., 2018 ; BuonannoÂ etÂ al., 2011 ; GorjinezhadÂ etÂ al., 2017 ; TorkmahallehÂ etÂ al., 2017a ). Such studies primarily measured the concentrations and volatile mass distributions of aerosols, the size distribution and chemical composition of particles, and emission rates of (PM)2.5 . Measurements over several meals suggested that using a nonstick pan and proper cooker hood were feasible solutions for reducing (PM)2.5  emission rates. Indeed, fatty meat foods are a significant source of aerosol particles compared with vegetable foods, and the higher moisture content in vegetables is possibly the reason for the higher volatile fraction of aerosols. In addition to on-site measurements of cooking-generated fine particles ((PM)2.5  and (PM)10 ), a study on 30 residential and experimental buildings, that evaluated broiling fish/meat, frying eggs/meat, and preparing soups or toast as representatives of the eating habits of some Korean families, provided limited results (KangÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). For clarity, grilling prawns, corn, trout, beef, and pork as per Japanese cooking styles, without any additives and were generally measured in a home cooker; the (PM)2.5 -bound PAHs were evaluated based on particle size and concentration distributions (Saito etÂ al., 2014 ). As cooking activity is interlinked with the types of meals prepared, more representative measurement is necessary, as Japanese cooking includes a rich diet, which can lead to high emission rates. Interestingly, tests on different types of oil have also been studied, including those on pure oils (5) and frying simple food (3) (Fig.Â 4 ). Reports on pure oils contributed to the evaluation of the sensitivities of different oils in the heating process. Monitoring of volume-based particles has been proposed owing to emissions of different particle sizes after high-temperature decomposition (AmoueiÂ Torkmahalleh etÂ al., 2013 ; TorkmahallehÂ etÂ al., 2012 ), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) can form from a variety of different odor-chemicals (KatragaddaÂ etÂ al., 2010 ; YiÂ etÂ al., 2019 ; ZhangÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). The particle emissions of jointly released odor-chemicals in indoor and outdoor ambient environments have also been studied from frying beefsteak using four kinds of oil that can form mutagenic aldehydes in fumes (SjaastadÂ and Svendsen,Â 2008 ). Measuring deep-fried single food items in different types of cooking oils provided a comparison for assessing if single food items could induce higher concentrations of volatile carbonyls (SeamanÂ etÂ al., 2009 ), and it was found that lower concentrations and emission rates of carbonyls occurred as non-vegetable products did not have their own oils, ultimately leading to the absorption of oil during deep-frying. More particles were emitted from stir-frying cabbage and potatoes than from pork and chicken, as large amounts of water were present on the surface of washed vegetables (WangÂ etÂ al., 2018c ). Studying the synergetic effects of influential factors can provides more in-depth insight. Thus, some studies attempted to measure synergetic effects to provide representative data. These studies attempted consider possible permutations in the cooking method, ingredient weight, type of meat, type of oil, and meat/vegetable ratio, which provided insights for future designs of the measurement of spices, kitchen utensils, fuel, and ventilation. In the orthogonal testing methods, well-surveyed Chinese-style cooking methods, oils, and kitchens were typically adopted during design phase (ChenÂ etÂ al., 2018 ; ZhaoÂ etÂ al., 2018 ). Five additives had different effects on the emissions of (PM)2.5  and UFPs from heated cooking oil (200Â Â°C). Among reduction processes, total particle emissions were observed for canola and soybean oil (AmoueiÂ Torkmahalleh etÂ al., 2013 ). VOCs and secondary organic aerosols (SOAs) are directly related to stir-frying spices in heating corn oil; primary emissions (i.e., aldehydes, methylpyrrole, dihydrohydroxymaltol, diallyldisulfide, and terpenes) could likely be attributed to differences in spice characteristics (i.e., type of microstructure, solution temperature, and duration), the cooking method, and the type of oil (LiuÂ etÂ al., 2017 ). A similar approach was used to measure particulate matter emissions by heating corn oil mixed with table salt using a pan on an electric stove with low ventilation and heating time (GorjinezhadÂ etÂ al., 2017 ). These designs used a single spice at high temperatures, and the amounts of different oils necessary for the partial or complete decomposition of a spice were evaluated. The robustness of the experimental design was confirmed by various methods, including grilling meat with salt and Western style dishes (AmoueiÂ Torkmahalleh etÂ al., 2018 ; O'LearyÂ etÂ al., 2019 ) (Fig.Â 5 a). However, the number of tests on cooking spices are too few to be used as representative experiments for different cooking styles. Finally, literature on ventilation was also organized. Here, four articles empirically explored, validated, or tested the effects of ventilation. These papers explored typical Chinese (DuÂ etÂ al., 2017 ) and Western style (O'LearyÂ etÂ al., 2019 ) dishes, heating water on different burners (SingerÂ etÂ al., 2017 ), boiling frozen broccoli, and frying four beef hamburger patties (DobbinÂ etÂ al., 2018 ) (Fig.Â 5 b). Two articles addressed the real-world household environment to explore stove fuels used for cooking-related activity (McCordÂ etÂ al., 2017 ; TagleÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). However, more real-world measurements in other areas are necessary to move beyond exploration, and to increase the adoption of survey and case study methods to test practical emissions. Natural gas and LPG are rapidly consumed in urban areas. Articles tested odor-chemicals from LPG without cooking activities (ShenÂ etÂ al., 2018 ), and those from typical cooking of different dishes (HuangÂ etÂ al., 2011 ). The distribution of indoor air pollution (IAP) concentrations was also evaluated by a Monte Carlo single-box mode combining stove emission rates with expected distributions of kitchen volumes and air exchange rates in a developing country, where characterization techniques could provide an insight into the evaluation of IAP concentrations and other model assumption (JohnsonÂ etÂ al., 2011 ) (Fig.Â 5 c). Overall, conducting representative studies on influential factors may require the measurement of volume-bound particles, particle-bound chemicals, and odor-emitting chemicals from specific study designs, real-world cooking activities, and simulations. According to studies on fuels and range hoods, clean fuels and range hoods were not used in some areas (CaiÂ etÂ al., 2019 ; ChampionÂ and Grieshop,Â 2019 ; DasÂ etÂ al., 2019 ; KalisaÂ etÂ al., 2018 ; ThompsonÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). Therefore, employing clean fuels and efficient range hoods in these regions can effectively lead to alleviating poverty as per SDG 1. Energy consumption was related to household energy efficiency as per calculated urban and rural activity data (BaumgartnerÂ etÂ al., 2019 ; ZhuÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). Moreover, recycling cooking oil waste inside the kitchen could potentially increase the effective energy consumption (AbedÂ etÂ al., 2018 ; DineshaÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). More studies have been conducted on commercial kitchens (14 articles) than on residential kitchens (6 articles), highlighting the primary role of studies on commercial kitchens in leading to reductions in cooking emissions. Outdoor emission levels around restaurants are also affected by meteorological conditions, such as temperature, humidity, and wind power. Gravimetric measurements, air exchange rates, and emission level calculations are indispensable phases of cooking studies. Air exchange rates are commonly characterized using sulfur hexafluoride tracer gas decay, and the pre- and post-weight filters acquired through 24Â h of equilibration at controlled temperature and humidity (Dacunto etÂ al., 2013 ; HammondÂ etÂ al., 2013 ; SheziÂ etÂ al., 2018 ). Strategies including the R programming language MALDI quant package can be used to smooth data and identify emission levels (ChanÂ etÂ al., 2018 ) (Fig.Â 6 b). Measuring emissions in hundreds of restaurants and households utilizing multipurpose and general cooking styles while considering the dynamic characteristics of volume-based particles, chemical characteristics of cooking emissions, and different meteorological conditions is challenging. This problem may be solved by developing pre-simulation models coupled with meteorological data to directly predict fresh emissions (TorkmahallehÂ etÂ al., 2017b ); however, many experimental parameters must be available online for researchers for this to be implemented. SDG 12 aims at ensuring responsible consumption and production, but these studies described in different regions suggested that cooking may lead to overconsumption in developed and developing countries with large populations and gross domestic products (AlexandratosÂ and Bruinsma,Â 2012 ; XueÂ etÂ al., 2017 ). The use of models could improve fuels and protective measures for special groups and is an attractive approach. Future work is required to measure real-world cooking emissions and evaluate various aspects of their health effects. SDG 2 includes combatting hunger at a regional level, providing food security, and improving nutrition (ChaudharyÂ and Krishna,Â 2019 ; HitajÂ etÂ al., 2019 ; XueÂ etÂ al., 2019b ). Establishing specific guidelines for residential and commercial kitchens is advocated for some countries. Most practiced cases (8 articles) use receptor models, followed by source models (5) and integrated models (1). These articles highlighted the primary role of source apportionment in quantifying secondary contributions and simulating emission reductions. A positive matrix factorization (PMF) receptor model quantifies the relative contributions of samples based on cooking factor fingerprints. The CMB equations relate the contributions to factor profiles (see Eq 3â1, 3â2), using a maximization-expectation (ME) algorithm to identify the most optimal factor (Eq 3â3). This model, with a specific horizontal resolution, was considered as a solution for simulating particle source contributions. To quantify (PM)2.5  source contributions at 23 sites in the San Joaquin Valley, California, estimation for cooking emissions were performed based on spatiotemporal samples and source marker (K + , OC, EC) following site characteristics and measured parameters for intensive observation periods. Spatial and temporal variations revealed that cooking factors were dominant at urban sites (ChenÂ etÂ al., 2007a ). Recently, based on samples collected over 12 months in three cities in California, one article simulated (PM)0.1  source contribution using molecular markers modified for volatility and reactivity. The results of this paper demonstrated that Western-style meat cooking has the largest contribution to (PM)0.1  OC across all sites (13â  29%) (XueÂ etÂ al., 2019a ). Particle source contribution is affected by anthropogenic activities, boundary layer dynamics and meteorological parameters. Such considerations led to investigations of the observed seasonality of (PM)10 -bound PAHs after detection and quantification over Kolkata and Darjeeling during three consecutive seasons (RayÂ etÂ al., 2017 ); the combination of the PMF simulation and these effects was an energetically favored process in terms of relative research. Furthermore, three articles measured the online chemical composition of OA using a high-resolution time-of-flight aerosol mass spectrometer (HR-ToF-AMS) to identify contributions (KaltsonoudisÂ etÂ al., 2017 ; RobinsonÂ etÂ al., 2019a ; ShahÂ etÂ al., 2018 ); among them, one article adopted innovative Land-use regression (LUR) models to calculate PMF factors. However, AMS-PMF apportionment measurements could be overestimated as all downward adjustments led to a proportional linear decrease in COA concentrations (OtsÂ etÂ al., 2016 ); the PMF was a bilinear model, and some of its factors could merge in case of strong correlations (DrosatouÂ etÂ al., 2019 ). Thus, PMF analysis was sensitive to factor number, implying that an increased factor number was not likely to produce new information relative to fewer factors (Dall'Osto etÂ al., 2015 ). For the effects of downward adjustments and factor number, modified molecular markers tracing factor contribution may be detailed enough to determine information regarding volatility and reactivity. Additionally, studies suggest that indoor characteristics could affect pollutant concentrations; consequently, sampling should be conducted by instruments absorbing large amounts of pollutants under different atmosphere conditions. In turn, these samples will have more than double the base characteristics, which will improve the relevance of models and provide a foundation for uncertainty analysis. Source-oriented models (SOM) have been applied to achieve cooking source apportionment coupled with weather research and forecasting (WRF) model in several ways. The first route was developed by the University of California DavisâCalifornia Institute of Technology (UCD/CIT), air quality chemical transport model (Eq 3â4). In depth, the total primary mass contributions from different sources in each particle size bin and primary OA mass concentrations can be apportioned by using artificial tracers and source-specific emission profiles, respectively (Eq 3â5). Source contributions from nine factors and background in San Joaquin Valley on January 4â6, 1996 and September 7â9, 1993, were calculated and characterized using the source-oriented UCD/CIT model based on photochemical activity and corresponding high airborne particulate matter concentrations. The comparison of the UCD/CIT and CMB model resulted in limited disagreement of primary (PM)2.5  mass, and high concentrations of primary (PM)2.5  mass not associated with cooking source (HeldÂ etÂ al., 2005 ). More specifically, the source contribution of primary and secondary particle (0.01â10Â mm) demonstrated that meat cooking led to the production of primary particles in urban centers. Of paramount importance in achieving this result of source contribution was that air quality and meteorological data from December 15, 2000 to January 7, 2001 was collected during the California Regional (PM)10 /(PM)2.5  Air Quality Study (CRPAQS). In the sensitivity analysis for nitrate formation and source apportionment, the effect of several parameters was explained, allowing improvements of the simulation process (YingÂ etÂ al., 2009 ). The University of California Davis_Primary (UCD_P) chemical transport model was applied to predict among the 151 (PM)2.5  and 71 (PM)0.1  source concentrations and seasonal profiles, with 4-km horizontal resolution, 7-year emission inventory and high-resolution meteorological data in California. The predicted results generally agreed with the measurements, and the spatial heterogeneity of different species led to a consideration of different PM fractions sizes (HuÂ etÂ al., 2014 ). Similarly, the contribution of ultrafine particulate matter (PM)0.1  relative to primary organic aerosols (POA) and secondary organic aerosols (SOA) in California was predicted with 4-km horizontal resolution, 9-year emissions inventory, and the 24- and 4-km hourly meteorological fields; important sources in summer were associated with meat cooking (HuÂ etÂ al., 2017 ). Integrated (PM)2.5  source apportionment could be developed by amalgamating the CMB, PMF, ME2 and WRF-CAMx models in the Yangtze River Delta region. To achieve this aim, offline (PM)2.5  filter samples collected from November 10, 2014 to January 14, 2015 were analyzed in laboratories to obtain the composition of ions, carbonaceous aerosols, and trace elements. Receptor-oriented source apportionment was performed based on CMBâ  PMFâ  ME2 model to categorize primary sources, secondary components and other unidentified sources. Source-oriented apportionment using WRF3.0 supplied the meteorological field, and Extensions-Particle Source Apportionment Technology (CAMx-PSAT version 6.40) tracked emission sources and quantified contribution factors resulting from the SMOKE simulation with categorized emissions data, characteristics and factor information (LiÂ etÂ al., 2018 ). The improved apportionments mentioned above are primarily applicable to online samples, analyzing particle-bound chemicals, innovating and integrating models. Which provides practical and promising strategy for simulating contributions to particles and O3.  The actual operation in any given setting varies, sometimes considerably, according to models, pollutant types, geography and meteorology, user needs, and many other factors. In mesoscale WRF simulation, errors are limited by referring to meteorology analyses of the National centre for Environmental Prediction (NCEP) Global Forecast System (GFS) (ConibearÂ etÂ al., 2018 ). Additionally, the alternative community multi-scale air quality (CMAQ) model (Eq 3â7) is popularly used in source apportionments, and multiple numerical simulation methods based on the CMAQ model have been developed, such as response surface modeling (RSM) (XingÂ etÂ al., 2011 ). The updated RSM technique simulates the nonlinear relationship between ambient pollutants and their precursors and the contributions of multiregional sources (XingÂ etÂ al., 2018 , 2017 ).   4 Conclusions We systematically selected and analyzed 131 articles relative to cooking source studies in the context of sustainable development goals. The following conclusions were drawn.(1) Representative experiments have been practiced in studies of influential factors through measurements on cooking different dishes and real-world cooking activities. The major influential factors of cooking emission were fuels and ventilations, which highlighted promising research directions for SDG 1 and 7.  (2) The cooking emissions results suggested that researchers should carefully measure more commercial and residential kitchens with advanced test methods by considering food and kitchen types, instruments, and analysis methods. To provide more real-world cooking emissions, the limitations of large-scale measurements and countermeasures should be carefully considered when designing models for simulating fresh emissions.  (3) To reduce the levels of cooking emissions, studies on emission factor could be directed at the consideration of volume-bound particles, particle- and odor-bound chemicals, and the development of instantaneous emission factors.  (4) More typical estimations for health effects need to be conducted to reduce personal exposures. Reducing exposure to cooking source is critical for achieving SDG 3, 5 and 8. Suitable technologies and programmatic approaches are required to remove or transform pollutants in specific environment.  (5) These source apportionments provided a means for researchers, producers and policy makers to use simulations to form their decisions; real-time emission inventories and optimized algorithms can be applied to more integrated or improved models.    One distinct limitation of this review is the number of articles on emission factors and health effects. That is likely to partly be the result of the retrieval of studies from a database on environmental sciences. Moreover, although discussed source apportionments provide policy guidance, control technologies have not been organized and analyzed. The simulation of fresh emissions is typically performed during the development stage of model; therefore, uncertainty exists regarding the validity of the expected results and the large-scale adoption potential of models. One methodological limitation was that only scientific articles conducted in English were considered, excluding articles in Chinese that may provide important contributions. There was researcher bias when organizing articles, notably for studies of influential factors. Consequently, some of these results may be biased in the partitioning of research objectives. Source apportionments also reflected contributions from other anthropogenic sources (in particular outdoor emission sources), implying that a comprehensive discussion should be conducted on this in the future.
